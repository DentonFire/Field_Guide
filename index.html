Why This Guide Exists

A real guide to Denton's natural hazards.

Most snakes in Denton are harmless. Most spiders you will see will not hurt you. Most plants in your yard are fine to walk past. But some are not, and knowing the difference makes the difference. This guide shows you how to identify the real threats, what to do when things go wrong, and how to prevent encounters in the first place. Every species entry is grounded in a cited source.

If It Already Happened

Bite, sting, or exposure response.

If you are reading this because something just happened, start here. Move through the steps in order. Get to a hospital if a venomous bite is possible.

Snake Bite

Any bite where the species is unknown should be treated as venomous until proven otherwise at a hospital.

1 Move away from the snake

  • Get the person at least 20 feet back. Snakes can strike roughly a third of their body length.
  • Do not try to kill or capture the snake. Photograph it from a safe distance only if you can do so without delay.

2 Call 911 or get to an emergency room

  • Medical City Denton and Texas Health Presbyterian Denton are the closest EDs. Call ahead so antivenom can be prepared before arrival. Confirm antivenom availability with the receiving hospital.
  • If symptoms progress fast (rapid swelling, breathing difficulty, dizziness), call 911 instead of driving yourself.

3 Keep the person still and calm

  • Activity spreads venom. Have the person sit or lie down.
  • Keep the bitten limb level with the heart. Not above, not below.
  • Remove rings, watches, and tight clothing before swelling starts.
  • Mark the edge of swelling with a pen and note the time. Repeat every 15 minutes so the hospital can see progression.

4 Clean lightly and cover

  • Gently wash the bite with soap and water if available.
  • Cover with a clean, dry dressing. Do not apply pressure.

Do Not

  • Do not cut the bite or try to suck out venom. Makes tissue damage worse.
  • Do not apply a tourniquet. Concentrates venom and causes limb loss.
  • Do not apply ice. Cold makes tissue damage worse.
  • Do not give the person alcohol, caffeine, aspirin, or ibuprofen.
  • Do not pick up a dead snake. Muscle reflexes can still inject venom hours after death.

Insect Sting with Allergic Reaction

A normal sting causes local pain and swelling. Anaphylaxis is different and can kill within minutes.

1 Recognize anaphylaxis

  • Difficulty breathing, wheezing, or throat tightness
  • Swelling of lips, tongue, or face
  • Rapid pulse, dizziness, or fainting
  • Hives spreading beyond the sting site
  • Nausea, vomiting, or abdominal cramps

2 Act immediately if any of those appear

  • Call 911.
  • Use an epinephrine auto-injector (EpiPen) if prescribed. Inject into the outer thigh, hold 3 seconds, then call 911 even if symptoms improve.
  • Keep the person lying flat with legs elevated unless they are vomiting or having trouble breathing.
  • Do not give anything by mouth if they are having trouble swallowing.

3 For a normal sting without allergic reaction

  • Remove the stinger by scraping with a credit card. Honey bees leave stingers; wasps do not.
  • Wash with soap and water. Apply a cold compress.
  • Take an oral antihistamine for severe itching.
  • Watch for 30 minutes for any spreading symptoms.

Plant Exposure (Poison Ivy, Oak, Sumac)

The oil (urushiol) causes the reaction. Speed matters because the oil bonds to skin within 10 to 20 minutes.

1 Within the first 10 minutes

  • Wash the exposed skin with cool water and dish soap or a product like Tecnu or Zanfel.
  • Scrub gently. Do not use hot water, which opens pores and spreads the oil.
  • Wash clothing separately in hot water. Wipe down gear, shoes, and tools with rubbing alcohol.
  • Wash pets. Urushiol rides on fur without affecting the pet.

2 If the rash develops (12 to 72 hours later)

  • Apply calamine lotion or hydrocortisone cream.
  • Take an oral antihistamine for itching at night.
  • Cool compresses reduce swelling.
  • Do not scratch. Breaking blisters increases infection risk, not rash spread.

3 Seek medical care if

  • The rash is on the face, near the eyes, or covers more than 25% of the body
  • Fever develops, or the rash shows signs of infection
  • You have difficulty breathing (possible from burning urushiol smoke)
  • You have a history of severe plant-exposure reactions

The Species You Need to Know

Venomous snakes of North Texas.

Texas has four groups of venomous snakes. All four occur across North Texas. Three are pit vipers (copperheads, cottonmouths, rattlesnakes). One is an elapid (coral snake). The pit vipers share identifying traits. The coral snake is in a category of its own.

Pit viper markers, from a safe distance

  • Triangular, arrowhead-shaped head distinct from the neck
  • Vertical, slit-shaped pupils
  • Heat-sensing pit between eye and nostril
  • Thick, heavy body relative to total length
  • A single row of scales on the underside of the tail

Non-venomous snake markers

  • Rounded, spoon-shaped head, similar width to the neck
  • Round pupils
  • No heat pit between eye and nostril
  • Slender body in proportion to length
  • Divided double row of scales under the tail

Don't Kill a Snake You Don't Need To

Venomous vs. harmless look-alikes.

Most snakes in Denton are not venomous. Killing a harmless snake removes a natural rodent predator from your yard. Learn the key differences so you can tell them apart without getting close.

Eastern Copperhead

Texas Rat Snake

Eight Legs, Six Legs, Many Problems

Insects and arachnids.

Dangerous spiders are uncommon but present in Denton. Stinging insects are everywhere. Ticks carry disease. Fire ants are aggressive. Knowing which is which and how to respond matters.

Most Bites Are Preventable

Prevention that works.

Almost all snakebites in Texas are defensive. The snake was surprised, cornered, or stepped on. Prevention is about not surprising or cornering a snake in the first place. Same principle applies to most wildlife hazards.

Remove snake habitat

  • Keep grass short. Tall grass hides snakes and invites prey like rodents.
  • Stack firewood at least 18 inches off the ground, away from the house.
  • Remove brush piles, leaf piles, and debris.
  • Seal gaps under sheds and decks with hardware cloth.
  • Fix foundation gaps larger than 1/4 inch.

Control the food chain

  • Secure trash in closed containers.
  • Use squirrel-proof bird feeders. Spilled seed brings rodents, rodents bring snakes.
  • Clean under fruit trees regularly.
  • Treat fire ant mounds with bait.
  • Clean gutters. Standing water breeds mosquitoes.

Work safely outside

  • Wear closed-toe shoes or boots, not sandals, for yard work.
  • Wear gloves when reaching into mulch, compost, or dense plantings.
  • Use a long stick to probe before reaching into brush or under structures.
  • Watch where you step, especially around woodpiles, AC units, and landscape rocks.
  • Do not reach into openings you cannot see into.

Manage plants

  • Identify poison ivy on your property and remove it safely.
  • Dispose of urushiol-bearing plants in sealed bags, never burn them.
  • Wash tools and gloves after contact with suspect plants.
  • Remove oleander if small children or chew-happy pets have access.
  • Keep vines off fences and tree trunks where they are easier to avoid.

Before you go

  • Check the forecast. Rain pushes snakes to higher ground; heat drives them to shade.
  • Tell someone your route and return time.
  • Bring a charged phone, water, and basic first aid.
  • Wear closed-toe shoes or boots. Hiking boots reduce ankle bites.
  • Long pants in snake country. Denim reduces fang penetration.

On the trail

  • Stay on marked paths. Cutting through brush is where bites happen.
  • Step onto logs and rocks, not over them. Snakes rest on the far side.
  • Use a trekking pole to probe ahead in tall grass or brush.
  • Check logs and flat rocks before you sit.
  • Do not put hands where you cannot see.

Tick prevention

  • Treat clothing and boots with permethrin. Not on skin.
  • Apply DEET or picaridin repellent to exposed skin.
  • Tuck pants into socks or boots in heavy grass.
  • Wear light-colored clothing to spot ticks.
  • Full-body tick check after every outing: ankles, knees, waistband, armpits, scalp.

Plant awareness

  • Identify poison ivy in all four seasons. Bare winter vines still have urushiol.
  • Avoid touching any unidentified plant.
  • Do not eat wild plants without certain identification.
  • Keep dogs leashed. They do not react to urushiol but carry it home.
  • If you brush a suspect plant, wash skin as soon as possible.

Identify water snakes

  • Most water snakes in Denton are harmless diamondback or blotched water snakes.
  • Cottonmouths are present. Less common in urban ponds.
  • Swimming style: cottonmouths ride high at a 45-degree angle, water snakes swim mostly submerged.
  • Both will defend themselves if cornered. Leave them alone.
  • Do not try to kill a snake in the water. You cannot ID it at that distance.

Boating and fishing

  • Watch overhanging branches. Snakes rest on limbs and occasionally drop.
  • Check coolers, gear bags, and life jackets stored outdoors.
  • Do not reach under docks, boat ramps, or into brush at the waterline.
  • Tell someone your fishing location and return time.
  • Keep first aid supplies on board.

Swimming safety

  • Check water conditions. Flood-swollen creeks wash snakes and hazards downstream.
  • Avoid swimming near heavy brush at the waterline.
  • Water hemlock grows at pond and creek edges. Never chew plant stems.
  • Stay out of water during blue-green algae blooms.
  • Life jackets for kids and weak swimmers.

Dock and boat ramp safety

  • Wear closed-toe shoes walking to the dock. Snakes sun on warm boards.
  • Move slowly. Watch your step.
  • Extra caution at dawn and dusk during hot months.
  • Check boat interiors before entering if stored uncovered.
  • Do not pile gear near the water. Creates cover that attracts snakes.

Seal entry points

  • Check weather stripping on garage and house doors.
  • Seal gaps around pipes entering walls.
  • Install door sweeps on garage and exterior doors.
  • Cover vents and chimneys with quarter-inch hardware cloth.
  • Close crawl space vents with mesh that allows airflow.

Garage and shed

  • Keep doors closed, not propped open.
  • Do not stack boxes directly on the floor.
  • Tap boots and gloves before putting them on if they have been sitting.
  • Check pool floats, pool noodles, and coiled hoses.
  • Use a flashlight when reaching into dark corners.

Spider prevention

  • Shake out shoes stored in the garage before wearing.
  • Check bedding in guest rooms or stored furniture.
  • Remove clutter from closets and storage spaces.
  • Seal cardboard boxes. Brown recluses favor them.
  • Vacuum corners, under furniture, and behind appliances regularly.

Pest prevention

  • Fix leaky faucets and wet spots that attract insects.
  • Use door sweeps to keep out ants, wasps, and scorpions.
  • Empty standing water in flower pot trays, pet bowls, and outdoor toys.
  • Store food in sealed containers.
  • Clean grease and crumbs from kitchen surfaces.

Teaching kids

  • "See a snake? Step back. Tell an adult." Age-appropriate for preschool.
  • Never let a child reach into rocks, holes, or brush piles.
  • Teach the coral snake color rule: red and yellow touching means stop.
  • Teach "leaves of three, let it be" for poison ivy.
  • Do not eat anything from the yard or trail without an adult's permission.

Playground safety

  • Walk the play area before use. Look for wasp nests, fire ant mounds, snake sign.
  • Check under equipment for ground wasp nests.
  • Inspect slides and tire swings for spider webs.
  • Keep shoes on at parks, even at splash pads.
  • Do not let kids sit directly on the ground for long stretches in warm months.

Dogs

  • Rattlesnake avoidance training is available in DFW. Worth it for rural or trail-regular dogs.
  • Keep dogs leashed on trails. Off-leash dogs find snakes first.
  • Treat for ticks and fleas year-round.
  • Dogs rarely react to urushiol but carry the oil home on fur.
  • Know your nearest emergency vet.

Cats

  • Indoor cats are safer than outdoor cats. Single biggest wildlife safety factor.
  • Outdoor cats bring snakes, birds, and rodents to porches.
  • Every part of a lily plant is toxic to cats.
  • Oleander, pokeweed, and sago palm are all toxic to cats.
  • Flea and tick control is important for indoor/outdoor cats.

Quick Field Card

Snake bite: Move away, keep still, call 911, mark swelling edges, get to a hospital.

Bee or wasp sting: Remove stinger by scraping. Wash. Cold pack. Watch 30 minutes.

Anaphylaxis: EpiPen in outer thigh. Call 911 immediately.
